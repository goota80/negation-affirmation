Chapter 1 — Ethiopian Zionism: Origins and its Establishment
The Kebra Negast

In the 14th century, the ideological foundation for modern Ethiopia was written. This text, borne in the ancient clerical language of Ge'ez, was known as the Kebra Negast, or the Glory of Kings. The text frames "Ethiopia" not simply as the highlands of Abyssinia but as a biblical and universal land of Cush, an ideological move that would later be wielded to justify its claims over the entire Horn of Africa. Thus, the Kebra Negast would be the pillar of Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity and modern imperial "Ethiopia," facilitating the fusion of church and state and serving as the basis for Abyssinia's later crusades that would lead to the formation of the Ethiopian Empire atop a prison house of nations.

The book in essence names the leaders of "Ethiopia" as the direct descendants of the biblical Ancient Israel. As the tale is told, on a delegation to Israel, Queen of Sheba of Ethiopia was wooed by King Solomon of Israel, and they had a night of passion. Back in Ethiopia, she had his child. That child would be named Menelik, and when he grew up, Menelik traveled to Jerusalem to meet his father, Solomon. Solomon recognized Menelik as his favorite child, and Menelik returned to Ethiopia with the finest, most prestigious of Israelites accompanying him. Among his congregation were Hebrew priests who smuggled with them the Ark of the Covenant — a gold chest that supposedly bore the very tablets that God himself inscribed the Ten Commandments in. That ark is supposedly held today in the Cathedral of St. Mary of Zion in Axum, located in modern Ethiopia's Tigray province in the north. Within the cathedral, priests trade off standing guard over the ark while a constant stream of poor rural people and tourists huddle outside to bask in its glow. Conveniently, nobody besides the priests is allowed to enter the church to look at the ark, but churches across Ethiopia are constructed as "models" of Solomon's temple and contain within them symbolic replicas of the ark.

Thus, Ethiopia's supposed relationship to Ancient Israel is consecrated through Menelik I, the Ark of the Covenant, its Orthodox churches made in the image of Solomon's temple with replicas of the ark, and not to mention the distinctive Hebrew traditions, practices, and language imbued within Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity itself. Placed alongside an excerpt from the Jewish text Midrash Tanchuma, it's clear how Ethiopia sought to situate itself at the center of the universe:

"As the navel is set in the center of the human body, so is the land of Israel the navel of the world; it is situated in the center of the world, and Jerusalem in the center of the land of Israel, and the Temple in the center of Jerusalem, and the Holy of Holies in the center of the Temple, and the Ark in the center of the Holy of Holies, and the foundation stone before the Holy of Holies, because from it the world was founded."

Midrash Tanchuma

The Ethiopian Jews

All of this is to say nothing of the Beta Israel community, the Ethiopian Jews who reside near Gondar in Ethiopia's Amhara province in the north. The Beta Israel are both a source of legitimacy to Ethiopia's claims to descendance from Ancient Israel and a source of revulsion, given that Jews are seen by Ethiopian Orthodox Christians as the betrayers of Christ.

To the Ethiopian Zionists, the existence of Ethiopia as an Orthodox Christian nation is proof that the Jews of Ancient Israel were "restored" and perfected through Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity. So while the European Christian Zionists who played a crucial role in establishing Israel in Palestine believed the Jews must return to that Israel to eventually convert to Christianity to usher in the second coming of Christ, Ethiopian Zionism already proclaims itself the final, perfected version of Israel — Zion not in waiting but already fulfilled.

Yet, as the Beta Israel endured brutal conditions in Ethiopia, many of them looked to the Yishuv (early Jewish settlements in Palestine) and later a newly emerging Israel in occupied Palestine as their promised land and began to flock there, undermining the Ethiopian regimes' narrative of being the true Zionist entity and bolstering Israel's claims to being the land of return for the world's Jews. Eventually, when its back was to the wall owing to the national liberation struggles, Ethiopia would begrudgingly use the Beta Israel as a bargaining chip. So long as it was done in secret to not tarnish Ethiopia's reputation as descended from Solomon, Israel could receive loyal new black Jewish settlers. In the act itself, Ethiopia could launder the racial cover it receives on the world stage to Israel, and conveniently at a time when the Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO) had made inroads into Black America. In turn, the Beta Israel would settle Palestinian lands, fill the ranks of the occupation army, as well as take up highly visible political and cultural posts. In return, Ethiopia could receive weaponry, security expertise, and economic aid to crush the rebellion of the oppressed nations within.

This symbiosis played out most notably in the 1980s when the Ethiopian military government was in existential crisis from the mounting national liberation struggles of the Eritreans, the Oromo, and the Tigray. Within this, it on multiple occasions agreed to exchange tens of thousands of Ethiopian Jews for Israeli assistance. Ironically, the brutal, racist Israeli state would come to treat the Beta Israel just as poorly as the Ethiopians did. However, given that many of the Beta Israel were unquestionably loyal to the Israeli Zionist cause, it meant that even if the majority of them faced abject conditions — from forced sterilizations, to staggering poverty, and rampant police killings — their propensity for revolt was quite low. Thus, while Ethiopia could acquire the means to extinguishing the oppressed — primarily the Oromo — in Israel, the Beta Israel were a useful dumping ground for antagonisms borne from the struggle of the oppressed — in particular the Palestinians. The two entities quite literally reinforced each other.

Wielding the Kebra Negast

In the centuries following the Kebra Negast, the Abyssinian aristocracy, priests, and nuns saw themselves as the descendants of the Israelites. Kings and emperors saw themselves as Kings of Zion, and their names were nods to their descendance from Ancient Israel. For example, the most known king of the so-called Solomonic Dynasty (13th–16th century) was Yitzhak, who in the Hebrew Bible was one of the founding figures of Judaism. And centuries later, there was Menelik II, the emperor that carried out the European-backed crusades of the Oromo, Somali, Sidama, and others at the end of the 19th century. The significance of him bearing the name of Menelik was that, while wiping out the people in the south and east and mass converting the survivors from their indigenous religions and Islam to Orthodox Christianity, he was staking his claim to being the direct descendant of Menelik I, the prized child of King Solomon of Ancient Israel. Thus, before the genocide and ethnic cleansing of Palestinians by Jewish Zionism was the genocide and ethnic cleansing of the Oromo and others by Ethiopian Zionism.

Chapter 2 — Tewodros' Ambitions

Though Abyssinia had all the makings of Zion, the one thing it lacked was direct control over the holy city of Jerusalem, where Ancient Israel is said to have been. Situated at the opposite end of the Red Sea, Jerusalem of Palestine was under control of the Ottoman Empire. For centuries, Abyssinian monks inhabited the Dir-al-Sultan monastery there, which served as a powerful symbol of Abyssinia's historic ties to the city. However, in its weaker years, a plague wiped out the monks, and the Egyptian Coptic monks in the neighboring monasteries moved in and laid claim to the Dir-al-Sultan. Upon inheriting a weak kingdom, Emperor Tewodros, in reviving vigor around the Kebra Negast, sought to reclaim the Dir-al-Sultan, to which the Egyptians refused. This was considered a great humiliation and drove Tewodros' burning desire to take Jerusalem by force. In a letter to Queen Victoria in 1862, he wrote: "When I shall have conquered the Muslims… I will go to Jerusalem to restore the tomb of Christ to my authority."

Before Tewodros could even dream of Jerusalem, he had to get through the Muslims in the immediate vicinity — that being the Oromo, the Somali, the Afar, and others — who stood between him and his imperial fantasy. Tewodros, however, could not make it past the Oromo. In the midst of his wars against them, he petitioned Queen Victoria for military assistance. He received no response as the Crown at the time sought appeasement with the Ottomans rather than enable Tewodros in eventual direct confrontation with them. To force their hand, Tewodros imprisoned missionaries and representatives of the British government. In response, a British expedition led by General Richard Napier freed the hostages by force and imprisoned Tewodros. Shortly after, Tewodros committed suicide. His dream was to remain unrealized.

The Abyssinians saw this incident as a great betrayal by the British. Their aspirations to build the empire and inability to see it through on their own meant that, for the next century, they would continually have to look to Britain and the other powers for support on the grounds that, among other things, it was the true Zionist entity. In the meantime, subsequent emperors and rulers would have to abandon Jerusalem as an earthly concept and remain content with Jerusalem not being under its direct control. So they instead opted for a 'holy Jerusalem,' seeking to emanate soft power by shoring up its monastic presence in Jerusalem to lend credence to its claims of having holy ties to the city. Here, a succession of regimes built Solomonic churches, monasteries, and many other buildings in Jerusalem on what today is known as "Ethiopia" Street, which is a lasting imprint of their claim to being the true Zionist entity.

The fate of who held the keys to the Dir-Al-Sultan, however, remained in flux, with decades of diplomatic struggle unfolding around the question. Finally, in 1967, just as Ethiopia, with the unparallelled assistance of the Israeli counterinsurgency and American demolitions experts, suppressed the Oromo Macha-Tulama movement in the town and the Oromo-Somali Bale Revolt in the country, Israel defeated Egypt, Syria, and Jordan and captured East Jerusalem and the entire West Bank in the Six-Day War. Upon capture, it physically changed the locks of the Dir-Al-Sultan, expelled the Egyptian monks, and handed the keys to the Ethiopian Orthodox church. This cemented the modern political alliance between the Ethiopian and Israeli Zionist projects. By doing so, the two didn't just resolve a monastic dispute, but they through military conquest of Palestinian land on one hand and the defeat of a massive Oromo revolt on the other consecrated formal, begrudged unity between the Ethiopian myth of Solomonic descent and the Israeli Zionist myth of Jewish return while on the other side it fused the fate of the Oromo and Palestinian nations. Meanwhile, capturing Jerusalem brought sheer ecstasy for the Israeli Zionist project and gave it a base to further expand its territorial ambitions — while in Ethiopia, its access to the Dir Al Sultan could further cement its spiritual claims to being descended from Ancient Israel. Thus, when Israel was strengthened, it only legitimized Ethiopia more. Evidently, it was in Ethiopia and the Israelis' utmost interests to band together to crush the Oromo and Palestinians.
